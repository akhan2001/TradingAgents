## GOODWILL IMPAIRMENT: NOT A SIDE RISK, A CENTRAL RISK

The aggressive analyst claims goodwill is "only impaired if the underlying acquisition fails." This is technically true but misses the actual point: goodwill impairment is a *binary outcome*, and the conditions for impairment are not as dramatic as the phrase "fails" suggests.

Goodwill is tested for impairment whenever the fair value of the acquired unit falls below its carrying value. FPS carries $516.6M in goodwill on a balance sheet with only $446.5M in stockholders' equity. The goodwill represents the premium paid for recent acquisitions—essentially, the market's expectation of future cash flows above book value.

If those future cash flows underwhelm—not necessarily "fail," but just underwhelm—the goodwill gets impaired. And an impairment of even 30% of goodwill ($155M) would wipe out more than one-third of stockholders' equity in a single charge.

Here's the risk: the company is now carrying $816.9M in total intangible assets (44% of the balance sheet). If confidence in the acquisition integration falters, or if market conditions deteriorate, or if customer concentration becomes an issue, that intangible value can evaporate very quickly.

The aggressive analyst dismisses this by saying Q1 2026 profitability "suggests" the acquisition is working. But Q1 is one data point. The acquisition integration has not yet faced a full business cycle. It hasn't faced a recession. It hasn't faced major customer loss. Until those tests pass, the goodwill remains a loaded gun on the balance sheet.
