**Recommendation**: Underweight

**Rationale**: Both sides made substantive arguments, so let me walk through how I weighed them.

The bull's strongest points center on the AI infrastructure tailwind, the post-integration margin recovery in Q1 2026, and the forward PE of ~46x being relatively reasonable for a company growing revenue at 103% YoY. The Jefferies upgrade to a $56 PT and the clear operational improvement trend from Q2 2025's trough to Q1 2026's $39.3M operating income are genuine positives that can't be dismissed.

That said, the bear landed the more decisive punches on several critical issues:

**The forward EPS math doesn't close.** The bull's own sustainability arithmetic — $1.5B annualized revenue × 10.4% operating margin × ~25% tax rate − ~$53M interest expense — yields roughly $80–90M in net income, not the $290M embedded in the $1.19 forward EPS consensus. That's a $200M+ gap the bull never adequately explained. This is the central flaw of the bull thesis.

**Balance sheet risk is real, not cosmetic.** Net debt/EBITDA of ~3x with negative tangible book value and $578M in long-term debt leaves zero margin for error. The bear's point that leverage metrics deteriorate quickly if growth moderates even to 20–30% YoY is arithmetically sound.

**Free cash flow is essentially zero.** FY 2025 FCF was -$39.1M, and Q1 2026's +$0.9M FCF — after $25.5M in working capital drag — is not the evidence of financial health the bull portrays. The company is burning cash at the business level while reporting accounting profits.

**Sentiment is a contrarian warning.** Zero bearish voices on StockTwits, retail FOMO language ("$100 by end of summer"), and a reactive analyst upgrade following a 31% rally in a month are textbook late-cycle signals. The bull acknowledged this as a "yellow flag" but then dismissed it — the bear was right to flag it prominently.

**Goodwill impairment risk is underappreciated.** $816.9M in goodwill (44% of total assets) with barely any EBITDA headroom above the implied acquisition price creates asymmetric write-down risk in years 2–4, which is precisely the typical window for M&A disappointment.

Where I don't fully side with the bear: the valuation scenarios the bear presented ($25–35 downside target) are too aggressive in the base case. The AI infrastructure demand is real, Q1 2026 margins are genuine, and the company isn't in imminent distress. However, the risk/reward at current prices is clearly skewed negative, and the forward earnings assumptions are not credible without much stronger margin expansion evidence.

Bottom line: the bear's structural arguments — forward earnings gap, negative FCF, leverage with no buffer, and frothy sentiment — outweigh the bull's momentum and narrative arguments. This is not a Sell (the business itself is functioning), but it is not a stock to add to at these levels. Trimming exposure is the prudent call.
